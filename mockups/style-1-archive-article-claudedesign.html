On the Taxonomy of Dreams

Every commonplace book I have kept since university has a section for dreams, and every one of those sections has, at some point, tried and failed to sort its entries into categories. This is another attempt, made with the mild suspicion that the failure is the actual finding.

The obvious first cut is by content: dreams about falling, dreams about teeth, dreams about being back in a building that no longer exists in the shape it appears in. This is the taxonomy every dream dictionary uses, and it is almost useless, because the content of a dream is the least stable thing about it. The same falling dream means something different at twenty-two, stressed about an exam, than it does at thirty-eight, stressed about nothing you could name.

A second attempt, by texture

A more promising cut sorts by texture rather than content: dreams that feel like memory, dreams that feel like fiction, dreams that feel like a held breath. Under this system, two dreams with nothing in common on the surface, one about a childhood house and one about an unfamiliar coastline, can end up in the same drawer, because both carry the same quality of having already happened to someone, somewhere, before you got there.

The dream does not announce which drawer it belongs in. You find out afterward, usually while doing something else entirely, and usually too late to write it down correctly.

What I keep running into is that any taxonomy stable enough to be useful stops describing dreams and starts describing the person doing the sorting. The categories say more about what I am currently worried about, or currently reading, or currently trying to avoid thinking about during the day, than they say about whatever is actually happening in sleep. A taxonomy of dreams turns out to be, mostly, a taxonomy of the waking hours that produced them.

Where this leaves the project

I have not abandoned the commonplace book's dream section, and I do not intend to. But I have stopped expecting the categories to hold for more than a season at a time, and started treating each new attempt at sorting as its own small piece of evidence, useful mainly for what it reveals about the sorter rather than the sorted. That, at least, is a taxonomy that keeps working.
